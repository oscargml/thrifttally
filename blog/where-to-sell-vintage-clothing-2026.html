Where to Sell Vintage Clothing Online in 2026

Updated July 2026 · 7 min read

Vintage resale isn't a niche hobby anymore — it's a genuine category with its own platforms, audiences, and fee structures. Which one is "best" depends entirely on what's in the bag, not on which app has the most downloads.

The short version: eBay for broad reach and anything collectible, Etsy for curated vintage with buyer intent, Depop for Gen Z fashion at zero commission, Poshmark for higher-end clothing with an active resale community, and Facebook Marketplace for anything too bulky or fragile to ship. Here's the case for each.

eBay — the widest net

eBay's real advantage isn't price, it's reach and format flexibility. You can list a single well-photographed 1970s band tee at a fixed price, or put a genuinely rare piece up for auction and let collectors bid it past what you'd have guessed. Casual sellers get up to 250 free listings a month, and the final value fee sits around 13.25% for most categories before payment processing.

Use it when: the item could appeal to a collector, you're not sure what it's worth and want the market to tell you, or you're selling a mix of categories and don't want to manage five different shop profiles.

Etsy — curated buyer intent

Etsy's vintage category has one hard rule: items need to be at least 20 years old to be listed as "vintage." That restriction is also what makes the traffic valuable — people arrive already searching for old and pre-owned goods, not comparing you against fast fashion. Fees run a $0.20 listing charge plus roughly 6.5% transaction and 3%+$0.25 processing, which sounds like a lot of small charges stacked up, but the buyer intent tends to offset it.

Use it when: the piece has a clear story or aesthetic (a specific decade, a specific silhouette), and you're willing to write a real description instead of a one-line listing.

Depop — zero commission, younger audience

Depop made a significant change recently: US and UK sellers now pay 0% seller commission, down from the old 10% cut — you only pay payment processing (roughly 3.3% + $0.45). Combined with its Instagram-style browsing and a buyer base skewing 18–35, it's currently one of the best-value platforms for streetwear, Y2K, and trend-driven vintage specifically.

Use it when: your inventory leans toward wearable trend pieces rather than investment vintage, and you're comfortable building a followed "shop" rather than one-off listings.

Poshmark — the social resale community

Poshmark's fee structure is blunt: a flat $2.95 on anything under $15, and a flat 20% on everything above that, with no separate listing or processing charge. That 20% is steep at higher price points, but the platform's "closet" and sharing culture drives real repeat engagement, and it performs well for designer and higher-end secondhand clothing specifically.

Use it when: you're selling recognizable, photographable brand names and don't mind participating in the platform's social selling mechanics (sharing others' listings, joining parties) to get visibility.

Facebook Marketplace — for the bulky and the local

Not every vintage find should go in a box. Large furniture, mirrors, and anything fragile or expensive to ship is usually better sold locally. Facebook Marketplace charges no fee at all on local pickup transactions, only a 5% fee on items you ship — making it the default for anything where shipping cost would eat the sale price anyway.

Niche and managed platforms worth knowing

For furniture, art, and higher-end antiques specifically, curated marketplaces like Chairish and Ruby Lane attract a more deliberate, higher-spending buyer than a general marketplace — worth the stricter listing standards if that's your category. If you'd rather skip listing entirely, managed platforms like thredUp handle photography and pricing for you in exchange for a smaller cut of the final sale.

How to actually decide

Start narrow. Most sellers do best picking two platforms — one broad (eBay or Etsy) and one fashion-specific (Depop or Poshmark) — rather than spreading thin across all five from day one. Learn each platform's listing quirks and photography expectations before adding a third.

Whichever you pick, run the actual numbers before you commit to a price — the gap between what a platform advertises and what you actually net can be a few dollars per item, which adds up fast at volume.
